In wireless communication systems, frequency synthesis techniques are widely used to generatestable, programmable carrier frequencies. Phase-locked loop (PLL) based frequency synthesizersare used in applications such as cellular communications, wireless local area networks (WLAN), Bluetooth devices, and GPS receivers. Programmable PLLs have become critical in supporting flexible, multi-standard wireless devices in the increasingly crowded 2.4 GHz Industrial, Scientific, and Medical (ISM) band. Within the 2.4 to 2.48 GHz frequency range, various wireless communication standards have become widely adopted including IEEE 802.11b/g/n/ax (Wi-Fi), Bluetooth, Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE), Zigbee, and other protocols. Among other challenges in designing PLLs is the challenge of covering a significant frequency range while achieving fine resolution in the frequency. The programmable frequency divider in the feedback path of a PLL determines the output and frequency range as well as how closely spaced the channels will be. Conventional counter-based dividers show limitations at high frequencies due to propagation delay and power consumption-induced frequency limits. The pulse-swallow design approach mitigates some of these problems through a high-speed dual-modulus prescaler, followed by lower-speed programmable counters. This architecture provides division of gigahertz range frequencies but also minimizes the portions of the circuitry that must be designed to operate at the higher gigahertz range (cost, complexity, and power).
